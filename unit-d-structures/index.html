Lesson 1

Types of Structures: Form, Function & Materials

Learn to identify different structural types, understand why materials matter, and compare natural and human-made structures.

7-D.17-D.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can describe and classify structures as shell, frame, or solid.
  • I can identify materials used in structures and explain why they were chosen.
  • I can compare natural structures with human-made structures.
  • I can explain how form follows function in structure design.

✅ Success Criteria

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to check off all of these:

  • I can name the 3 types of structures and give an example of each.
  • I can explain why a specific material was chosen for a given structure.
  • I can match a natural structure to a human-made equivalent.
  • I can explain what "form follows function" means with an example.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Hover over each term to see its definition.

🏗️ StructureStructure: Something with a definite shape, designed to support a load. Examples: bridges, buildings, skeletons. 🥚 ShellShell Structure: A hollow structure with a thin outer layer for both shape and strength. Examples: eggshell, helmet, dome. 🏗️ FrameFrame Structure: A skeleton of connected parts (beams, bars) with open spaces between. Examples: bicycle, skeleton, crane. 🧱 SolidSolid (Mass) Structure: Made entirely of material throughout. Strength comes from bulk. Examples: dam, brick wall, mountain. ⚖️ LoadLoad: The forces acting on a structure. Dead load = its own weight. Live load = additional forces (people, wind, snow). 🔩 CompressionCompression: A pushing force that squeezes a material. Concrete is strong in compression. 🧵 TensionTension: A pulling force that stretches a material. Steel cables are strong in tension.

🏛️ What is a Structure?

A structure is anything that has a definite size and shape and is designed to support a load. Structures are everywhere — from bridges to bones, eggshells to skyscrapers.

💡 All structures must support their own weight (dead load) plus any additional forces placed on them (live load).

📐 Three Types of Structures

TypeDescriptionExamples
🥚 ShellA hollow structure with a thin outer layer that provides both shape and strength. No internal framework.Eggshell, igloo, helmet, turtle shell, airplane fuselage, dome
🏗️ FrameA skeleton of connected parts (beams, bars, columns) that supports loads. Open spaces between parts.Bicycle frame, house framing, crane, spider web, human skeleton
🧱 Solid (Mass)Made of solid material throughout — strength comes from the bulk of the material itself.Brick wall, dam, mountain, sandcastle, concrete barrier

Yes! Many real-world structures combine types. A house has a frame (wood studs), covered with a shell (drywall/siding), sitting on a solid foundation (concrete). Understanding each type helps engineers design better, stronger, and more efficient structures.

🔩 Structural Materials & Properties

The choice of material depends on what the structure needs to do. Each material has unique properties.

🪨

Concrete

Strong in compression. Heavy, durable, fireproof. Used for foundations and dams.

🔩

Steel

Strong in tension & compression. Flexible, can rust. Used for bridges and skyscrapers.

🪵

Wood

Light and easy to shape. Burns, can rot. Used for houses and furniture.

🪟

Glass

Transparent, hard, brittle. Breaks under impact. Used for windows and displays.

🧪

Plastic

Light, flexible, waterproof. Not heat-resistant. Used for containers and toys.

🦴

Bone

Light but strong (nature!). Living tissue that heals. Used by animals for internal frames.

💡 Alberta Outcome 7-D.2: Identify materials used in structures and relate their properties to function.

Lesson 2

Forces on Structures: Loads, Stress & Stability

Understand what forces act on structures, how stress affects materials, and what makes structures stable or unstable.

7-D.37-D.4

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can describe the forces that act on structures (tension, compression, shear, torsion).
  • I can distinguish between dead loads and live loads.
  • I can explain how the centre of gravity affects stability.
  • I can identify causes of structural failure and how engineers prevent them.

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can classify forces as tension, compression, shear, or torsion.
  • I can calculate which loads are dead vs. live on a structure.
  • I can explain why a wider base makes a structure more stable.
  • I can predict which design changes would strengthen a weak structure.

📖 Key Vocabulary

↗️ TensionTension: A pulling force that stretches a material — like pulling a rope from both ends. ↙️ CompressionCompression: A pushing/squeezing force that shortens a material — like standing on a sponge. ↔️ ShearShear: A sliding force that pushes parts of a material in opposite directions — like cutting with scissors. 🔄 TorsionTorsion: A twisting force — like wringing out a towel. ⚖️ Centre of GravityCentre of Gravity: The point where an object's mass is balanced. Lowering it increases stability. 📦 Dead LoadDead Load: The permanent weight of a structure itself — walls, floors, roof.

💪 Internal Forces on Structures

Every structure has forces acting on it. Engineers must design structures to resist four main types of internal force:

↗️

Tension

Pulling apart — cables on a suspension bridge are under tension

↙️

Compression

Squeezing together — pillars supporting a roof are under compression

↔️

Shear

Sliding in opposite directions — scissors cutting paper use shear force

🔄

Torsion

Twisting — wringing a towel or a drill bit turning applies torsion

📦 Loads: Dead Load vs. Live Load

The load is the total force acting on a structure. There are two types:

TypeDescriptionExamples
Dead LoadPermanent weight of the structure itselfWalls, floor, roof, beams
Live LoadTemporary forces that changePeople, furniture, snow, wind, vehicles

💡 Key Idea: Engineers must calculate both dead and live loads to design structures that won’t collapse. A bridge must support its own weight PLUS the cars, trucks, and people crossing it.

⏸️ Pause and Check

  • Name the 4 internal forces that act on structures.
  • What’s the difference between a dead load and a live load?
  • Give an example of tension in a real structure.

Answers: Tension, compression, shear, torsion · Dead = permanent structure weight, live = temporary changing forces · The cables on a suspension bridge are pulled tight (tension)

⚖️ Stability & Centre of Gravity

A structure is stable when it resists tipping over. Stability depends on:

⬇️

Low Centre of Gravity

The lower the centre of mass, the harder it is to tip over (e.g., race cars are low to the ground)

↔️

Wide Base

A wider base gives more support area (e.g., the Eiffel Tower’s wide base)

⚖️

Symmetry

Balanced weight distribution prevents tipping

💡 Alberta Connection: Calgary’s Bow Tower is a 236m skyscraper designed to resist Alberta’s strong Chinook winds. Engineers used a triangular bracing system and ensured the centre of gravity stays low through weighted foundations, allowing it to flex slightly in high winds without structural damage.

⚠️ Structural Failure

Structures fail when forces exceed what they were designed to handle. Common causes:

📈

Overloading

Too much weight or force applied beyond the design limit

🔧

Material Fatigue

Repeated stress weakens materials over time (like bending a paperclip)

🌧️

Environmental Forces

Wind, earthquakes, flooding, ice can exceed design specifications

⏸️ Pause and Check

  • How does lowering the centre of gravity improve stability?
  • Name 3 causes of structural failure.
  • Why is a triangular shape often used in structural design?

Answers: A lower centre of gravity means the structure is harder to tip because weight is concentrated near the base · Overloading, material fatigue, environmental forces · Triangles distribute force evenly and can’t be deformed without breaking a side

External Forces & Environmental Factors

Explore how wind, earthquakes, snow, and water challenge structures — and how engineers design for them.

D.K.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can identify external forces that act on structures (wind, gravity, seismic, snow, water).
  • I can explain how environmental factors influence structural design.
  • I can describe how friction helps and hinders structures.

📖 Key Vocabulary

External Force: Forces from outside the structure — wind, gravity, earthquakes, snow, water
Dynamic Load: Forces that change over time — wind gusts, traffic, earthquakes
Seismic Force: Ground shaking from earthquakes that can topple structures
Friction: Force that resists sliding between surfaces — can help (grip) or hinder (wear)

🎬 Watch: Forces on Structures

🌪️ External Forces in Alberta

❄️ Snow Load: Alberta's heavy snowfalls create massive loads on roofs. Steep pitched roofs allow snow to slide off; flat roofs must be engineered for weight.
🌬️ Wind: Chinook winds in Southern Alberta can gust up to 160 km/h. Buildings need wind bracing and deep foundations.
🌍 Seismic: Western Alberta has moderate earthquake risk. The 1985 Rocky Mountain House earthquake was 4.6 magnitude.
💧 Water/Ice: Freeze-thaw cycles crack foundations and roads. Proper drainage and waterproofing are critical.

Properties of Materials

  • I can compare natural and synthetic materials.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Tensile Strength: How well a material resists being pulled apart (stretched)
Compressive Strength: How well a material resists being crushed (squeezed)
Flexibility: Ability to bend without breaking
Composite: Material made from two or more substances combined — e.g., fibreglass, reinforced concrete

  • I can identify different fasteners and their purposes.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Fixed Joint: Rigid connection that does not move — welding, rivets, glue
Flexible Joint: Connection that allows movement — hinges, ball-and-socket, pivots
Gusset: Triangular plate added to a joint to strengthen it
Fastener: Device used to connect parts — nails, screws, bolts, rivets, adhesives

📖 Key Vocabulary

Design Cycle: Identify need → Research → Design → Build → Test → Evaluate → Improve → Repeat
Safety Margin: Extra strength built into a structure beyond minimum requirements — a built-in buffer
Prototype: A test model built before the final version to identify problems
Constraint: Limitations on a design — budget, materials, size, regulations

Lesson 7

Structural Analysis & Failure

Analyze real-world structural failures and learn how engineers prevent them.

D.K.1D.K.2D.K.4

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can analyze real-world structural failures and identify causes.
  • I can describe modes of failure (fatigue, overload, foundation, corrosion).
  • I can explain how engineers prevent structural failure.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Fatigue: Weakening caused by repeated stress cycles over time — like bending a paper clip back and forth
Overload: When forces exceed what a structure was designed to handle
Corrosion: Gradual chemical destruction of materials — rust on steel, acid rain on stone
Resonance: When vibrations match a structure's natural frequency, amplifying them dangerously
