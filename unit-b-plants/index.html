🌍 Why Do Plants Matter?

Plants are the foundation of nearly every ecosystem on Earth. Without plants, life as we know it would not exist. They perform some of the most critical jobs in nature:

🫁

Oxygen Producers

Plants produce the oxygen we breathe through photosynthesis.

🍎

Food Source

All food chains begin with plants — they are the primary producers.

🏠

Habitat

Forests, grasslands, and wetlands provide shelter for countless animals.

🌡️

Climate Regulators

Plants absorb CO₂ and release O₂, helping regulate Earth's atmosphere.

👕

Fibre & Materials

Cotton, wood, linen, and rubber all come from plants.

💊

Medicine

Many medicines come from plants — aspirin was originally from willow bark!

💡 Alberta Outcome 7-B.1: Illustrate and explain the essential role of plants within the environment.

💡 Alberta Connection: Alberta's boreal forest is one of the largest intact forests on Earth, providing habitat for caribou, bears, and hundreds of bird species while absorbing millions of tonnes of CO₂ each year.

💧 What Do Plants Need to Survive?

Like all living organisms, plants have basic needs that must be met for survival. These needs are provided by both abiotic and biotic factors in the environment.

☀️

Sunlight

Energy source for photosynthesis. Different plants need different amounts of light.

💧

Water

Essential for transporting nutrients and for photosynthesis. Absorbed by roots.

🌬️

Carbon Dioxide

Plants absorb CO₂ from the air through tiny pores called stomata in their leaves.

🧱

Soil Nutrients

Minerals like nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium are absorbed through roots.

📐

Space

Room for roots to spread and leaves to capture sunlight without competition.

🌡️

Right Temperature

Too hot or too cold can slow or stop growth. Each species has a preferred range.

🔬 Photosynthesis: How Plants Make Food

Photosynthesis is the process plants use to make their own food. It happens mainly in the leaves, inside structures called chloroplasts.

The Photosynthesis Equation

☀️ Light + 💧 Water + 🌬️ CO₂ → 🍬 Sugar + 🫁 O₂

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Photosynthesis is the primary source of energy for almost all life on Earth. Plants capture the Sun's energy and convert it into sugar (chemical energy). This energy flows through the food chain when animals eat plants. Photosynthesis also produces the oxygen that most organisms need to breathe.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Hover over each term to see its definition.

🌱 GerminationGermination: The process where a seed begins to grow into a new plant when conditions (water, warmth, oxygen) are right. 🐝 PollinationPollination: The transfer of pollen from the male part (anther) to the female part (stigma) of a flower, enabling fertilization. 💨 Seed DispersalSeed Dispersal: The way seeds are moved away from the parent plant — by wind, water, animals, or explosive pods. 🌸 StamenStamen: The male reproductive part of a flower, made up of the anther (produces pollen) and filament (supports the anther). 🌺 PistilPistil: The female reproductive part of a flower, made up of the stigma (catches pollen), style, and ovary (contains ovules). 🍎 FertilizationFertilization: When pollen reaches the ovule inside the ovary, creating a seed that can grow into a new plant.

🌱 From Seed to Plant: Germination

Every plant begins as a seed. Inside each seed is a tiny embryo plant and a food supply. When conditions are right, the seed germinates — it breaks open and starts growing.

Seeds need three things to germinate:

💧

Water

Softens the seed coat and activates enzymes that start growth

🌡️

Warmth

Enzymes need the right temperature range to work properly

🌬️

Oxygen

Needed for cellular respiration — the energy that powers initial growth

💡 Key Idea: Notice that seeds do NOT need light to germinate — they start underground! Light becomes important once the first leaves emerge above the soil and need to begin photosynthesis.

⏸️ Pause and Check

  • What provides the initial energy for a germinating seed?

Answers: Water, warmth, oxygen · Seeds start underground where there's no light; they use stored food · The food supply stored inside the seed provides energy through cellular respiration

🐝 Pollination & Reproduction

For flowering plants to make seeds, they need pollination — the transfer of pollen from the stamen (male part) to the pistil (female part).

🔄

Self-Pollination

Pollen transfers within the same flower or same plant. Simple but less genetic variety.

🐝

Cross-Pollination

Pollen moves between different plants via insects, birds, wind, or water. Creates more genetic diversity.

After pollination, fertilization occurs: the pollen grows a tube down to the ovary, joins with the ovule, and a seed forms. The ovary often develops into a fruit around the seeds.

💡 Alberta Connection: Alberta's canola industry depends heavily on pollination. Canola fields across central and southern Alberta rely on both wind and insect pollinators. Alberta produces over 30% of Canada's canola — worth billions of dollars annually. Without effective pollination, this major crop wouldn't produce seeds.

💨 Seed Dispersal

Plants can't walk to a new location, so they've evolved amazing ways to send their seeds far away. This prevents overcrowding and competition.

💨

Wind

Dandelion puffs, maple keys, cottonwood — light seeds with wings or parachutes

🌊

Water

Coconuts float across oceans; willow seeds travel along rivers

🐿️

Animals

Burrs stick to fur; berries are eaten and seeds pass through digestive systems

💥

Explosive

Touch-me-nots and pea pods burst open, flinging seeds away

Answers: Self = within same plant, cross = between different plants via pollinators · Wind (dandelion), water (coconut), animal (burrs), explosive (pea pods) · Prevents overcrowding and competition for light, water, nutrients

Plant Classification & Adaptations

Learn how scientists classify plants and how plants have adapted to survive in extreme environments.

B.K.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can distinguish between vascular and non-vascular plants.
  • I can compare flowering and non-flowering plants.
  • I can describe plant adaptations to extreme environments.
  • I can identify common Alberta plants using their characteristics.

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can sort plants into vascular/non-vascular groups and explain the difference.
  • I can give 3 examples of plant adaptations and explain how they help survival.
  • I can identify at least 4 Alberta plants by their features.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Vascular: Has tubes (xylem/phloem) to transport water and nutrients — ferns, trees, flowers
Non-vascular: No transport tubes — mosses, liverworts. Must stay small and moist
Angiosperm: Flowering plant that produces seeds inside a fruit
Gymnosperm: Non-flowering plant with "naked seeds" — conifers like spruce and pine
Adaptation: A trait that helps a plant survive in its specific environment
Dichotomous Key: A tool that uses yes/no questions to identify organisms

🎬 Watch: Plant Classification

⏸️ Pause & Check: What is the key difference between vascular and non-vascular plants?

🌿 Vascular vs. Non-Vascular Plants

The most fundamental way to classify plants is by whether they have a vascular system — internal tubes for transporting water and nutrients.

Feature🌿 Vascular🟢 Non-Vascular
TransportXylem & phloem tubesOsmosis/diffusion only
SizeCan grow very tall (trees)Stay small (a few cm)
RootsTrue rootsRoot-like structures (rhizoids)
ExamplesFerns, grasses, trees, flowersMosses, liverworts, hornworts

🌸 Flowering vs. Non-Flowering Plants

Among vascular plants, there's another key split:

🌸 Angiosperms (Flowering) — Produce flowers and fruits. Seeds are protected inside a fruit. Examples: roses, wheat, apple trees, dandelions.
🌲 Gymnosperms (Non-Flowering) — Produce seeds in cones, not flowers. "Naked seeds." Examples: spruce, pine, fir, larch. Most Alberta conifer forests are gymnosperms.
⏸️ Pause & Check: Is a dandelion an angiosperm or gymnosperm? Why?

🏔️ Plant Adaptations to Extreme Environments

Plants have evolved incredible adaptations to survive in harsh conditions:

🏜️ Desert: Cacti have thick stems to store water, waxy coatings to reduce water loss, and spines instead of leaves to minimize evaporation.
💧 Aquatic: Water lilies have broad floating leaves to capture sunlight, air spaces in stems for buoyancy, and flexible stems to handle water currents.
🏔️ Alpine: Alpine plants grow low to the ground to resist wind, have hairy leaves for insulation, and bloom quickly during the short growing season. Example: alpine forget-me-not (Alberta's provincial flower).
🌲 Boreal (Alberta): Conifer needles have waxy coatings, small surface area, and a conical shape to shed snow — adaptations to Alberta's cold, snowy winters.

🌲 Alberta Plant Identification

Alberta is home to diverse plant communities. Here are some iconic species:

🌲 White Spruce — Vascular, gymnosperm. Sharp needles, cylindrical cones. Alberta's most common tree.
🍂 Trembling Aspen — Vascular, angiosperm. Round leaves that "tremble" in wind. Forms large clonal colonies.
🌺 Wild Rose — Vascular, angiosperm. Alberta's provincial flower. Pink petals, prickly stems.
🟢 Sphagnum Moss — Non-vascular. Found in Alberta's bogs. Absorbs 20× its weight in water.
🌾 Western Wheat Grass — Vascular, angiosperm. Important prairie grass. Deep roots prevent soil erosion.

🌱 The 4 Key Growth Factors

☀️ Light: Powers photosynthesis. More light = more food = more growth. Too little light → etiolated (pale, stretched) plants. Different plants need different amounts (shade vs. sun plants).
💧 Water: Essential for photosynthesis, nutrient transport, and cell structure (turgor pressure). Too little → wilting. Too much → root rot from lack of oxygen.
🌡️ Temperature: Enzymes work best in a specific range. Too cold → growth slows. Too hot → enzymes denature. Alberta's growing season is limited by cold winters.
🧪 Soil & Nutrients: NPK (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium) are the "big 3" nutrients. Nitrogen → leaf growth. Phosphorus → roots & flowers. Potassium → overall health. Soil type (sand, clay, loam) affects water retention and drainage.

Agriculture, Pests & Controls

📖 Key Vocabulary

Monoculture: Growing a single crop over a large area — efficient but risky
Pesticide: Chemical that kills pests (insects, weeds, fungi). Can harm non-target species
Biological Control: Using living organisms (predators, parasites) to control pests naturally
Organic Farming: Growing food without synthetic chemicals — uses compost, crop rotation, natural pest control
Crop Rotation: Changing which crop is grown in a field each season to maintain soil health
IPM: Integrated Pest Management — combining multiple strategies to minimize chemical use

🎬 Watch: How We Grow Food

🐛 Pest Control Methods

Pests (insects, weeds, fungi) can destroy crops. Farmers use different methods to control them:

🧪 Chemical Control: Pesticides (insecticides, herbicides, fungicides). Fast and effective but can harm beneficial insects (pollinators), contaminate water, and lead to pest resistance.
🐞 Biological Control: Introducing natural predators. Example: ladybugs eat aphids. Safer for ecosystems but slower and less predictable. Risk: the introduced species might become invasive.
🔄 Cultural Control: Crop rotation, companion planting, resistant crop varieties. Low cost, sustainable, but requires more planning and knowledge.
⚙️ IPM (Integrated Pest Management): Combines all methods — monitor first, use cultural and biological controls, and only resort to chemicals when necessary. Best practice for sustainable farming.

🌾 Monocultures: Efficient but Risky

Alberta's prairies feature massive monocultures — fields of a single crop (canola, wheat, barley).

✅ Advantages: Easy to plant, manage, and harvest with machines. Higher short-term yields. Lower labour costs.
❌ Risks: One disease can wipe out the entire crop. Soil nutrients get depleted. Heavy pesticide/fertilizer dependence. Reduces biodiversity.
⏸️ Pause & Check: Why is crop rotation an important alternative to monoculture?

Plants, People & Sustainability

Explore how humans have shaped plant species, the value of Indigenous plant knowledge, and the future of sustainable food.

B.K.4

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can explain how selective breeding has changed plant species.
  • I can describe Indigenous knowledge about plants and land stewardship.
  • I can explain the importance of sustainable forestry and agriculture.
  • I can evaluate trade-offs of different plant technologies.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Selective Breeding: Choosing plants with desired traits to reproduce over many generations
GMO: Genetically Modified Organism — genes directly altered in a laboratory
Sustainability: Using resources in a way that meets current needs without harming future generations
Food Security: Having reliable access to enough affordable, nutritious food
Forestry: Managing forests for timber, recreation, and ecosystem health
TEK: Traditional Ecological Knowledge — Indigenous understanding of ecosystems built over generations

🎬 Watch: How Humans Changed Plants

🌽 Selective Breeding: Then and Now

Humans have been changing plants for over 10,000 years through selective breeding:

🌽 Corn: Wild ancestor (teosinte) had tiny ears with few hard kernels. Through thousands of years of selection, farmers created modern corn with large, juicy ears.
🥦 Brassica Family: Broccoli, cauliflower, kale, Brussels sprouts, and cabbage all come from ONE wild mustard ancestor — different traits were selected in each case.
🌿 Alberta Canola: Canola was developed in the 1970s by Saskatchewan and Manitoba scientists from rapeseed — selectively bred to have low erucic acid and low glucosinolates, making it safe for eating.

🤝 Indigenous Plant Knowledge

Indigenous peoples across the prairies and boreal regions have deep knowledge of plants built over thousands of years:

🫐 Food Plants: Saskatoon berries, wild rice, wild turnip, labrador tea — harvested sustainably with protocols respecting the land.
💊 Medicine: Sweetgrass, sage, cedar, and tobacco are sacred medicines. Willow bark (containing salicylic acid — the basis for aspirin) was used for pain relief.
🔥 Land Management: Controlled burns (prescribed fire) were used to clear brush, promote new growth, attract game, and maintain healthy grassland and forest ecosystems.
⏸️ Pause & Check: How does Indigenous fire management benefit ecosystems?

🌲 Sustainable Forestry in Alberta

Alberta's boreal forest covers over 60% of the province. Sustainable forestry practices include:

🌱 Replanting: Forest companies must replant harvested areas. Alberta requires reforestation within 2 years of logging.
📐 Buffer Zones: Leaving strips of trees along rivers and streams to protect water quality and wildlife corridors.
🔄 Selective Harvesting: Cutting only certain trees instead of clear-cutting, allowing forest ecosystems to recover faster.

🏆 School Garden Sustainability Plan

Apply everything you've learned to design a sustainable school garden that addresses plant needs, growing conditions, pest management, and food security.

📋 Your Task

You are a student environmental consultant hired to design a school garden. Your plan must show mastery of plant biology, growing conditions, pest management, and sustainability.

📝 Complete all 5 sections below. Your answers will be saved automatically. When you're ready, click "Submit Performance Task" at the bottom.

1️⃣ Garden Design & Plant Selection

Choose at least 5 plants for your garden. For each, explain: Is it vascular/non-vascular? Angiosperm/gymnosperm? What are its needs (light, water, soil)? Why did you choose it?

2️⃣ Growing Conditions Plan

How will you ensure good growing conditions? Address: soil preparation, watering schedule, sunlight needs, temperature considerations for Alberta's climate.

3️⃣ Pest Management Strategy

What pests might affect your garden? Describe your IPM plan — at least 2 control methods and explain why you chose them over other options.

4️⃣ Sustainability & Food Security

How does your garden support sustainability? How could it address food security in your school community? Include at least one idea inspired by Indigenous plant knowledge.

5️⃣ Reflection & Connections

What was the most important thing you learned in this unit? How has your understanding of plants changed? What would you do differently if you could redesign your garden?

🏆 Submit Your Plan

Make sure all 5 sections are complete. Your teacher will review and provide feedback.
