Lesson 1

Making Inferences

Use clues in the text combined with your own background knowledge to understand what's happening "between the lines."

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can use comprehension strategies to interact and respond to texts.
  • I can make inferences by combining text clues with my own knowledge.
  • I can evaluate the effectiveness of comprehension strategies I use.
Comprehension Inference Text Evidence
Inference A conclusion you reach based on evidence and reasoning — reading "between the lines." Text Clues Specific words, phrases, or details in a text that help you figure out something the author doesn't directly say. Background Knowledge What you already know from your own experiences and learning that helps you understand new text. Explicit Information that is stated directly and clearly in the text. Implicit Information that is suggested or hinted at, but not stated directly.

📖 Read: Understanding Inferences

When you read, not everything is spelled out for you. Authors expect you to use clues they provide — along with what you already know — to figure things out. This is called making an inference.

The Inference Formula: Text Clues + Background Knowledge = Inference

Think of it this way: if you walk into your house and smell cookies baking, you infer that someone has been baking — even though nobody told you. You used a clue (the smell) plus your knowledge (cookies require baking) to draw a conclusion.

Explicit vs. Implicit Information

📢

Explicit

Stated directly in the text. You can point to the exact words.

🔍

Implicit

Not stated directly. You must infer using clues and your own knowledge.

Example: Making an Inference

"Marcus stared at the test paper on his desk. He tapped his pencil against the edge, then looked around the room. Everyone else was writing. He sighed and stared at question one again."

Text Clues: Marcus is staring, tapping his pencil, sighing, and looking at everyone else writing.

Background Knowledge: When people stare at a test and sigh, they're usually struggling.

→ Inference: Marcus is having difficulty with the test.

⏸️ Pause and Check

Read this passage: "Lena sat at the kitchen table, flipping through the pages of her math textbook. She chewed her lip and glanced at the clock — 9:47 PM. She rubbed her eyes and turned back to the problem."

What can you infer about Lena?

Inference: Lena has been studying for a long time and is getting tired. Text clues include the late time (9:47 PM), rubbing her eyes, and chewing her lip (stress/frustration). Background knowledge tells us these are signs of fatigue and difficulty.
