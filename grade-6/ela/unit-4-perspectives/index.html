Perspectives and Justice

Examine how different perspectives shape how we understand issues, events, and each other. Compare viewpoints, discuss ideas, and write persuasive responses.

Culminating Task: Write a persuasive response and participate in a Socratic dialogue.

Lesson 1

Walking in Someone Else's Shoes

Read paired texts showing different perspectives on the same situation. Learn to identify and compare viewpoints.

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can identify and compare different perspectives in texts.
  • I can explain why different people might see the same situation differently.
  • I can use text evidence to support my understanding of a perspective.
PerspectiveComparisonEmpathy
Perspective A person's point of view — how they see and interpret the world, shaped by their experience and background. Bias A tendency to favour one viewpoint over another, sometimes unfairly. Empathy The ability to understand and share the feelings of another person. Viewpoint The position from which someone observes or considers something.

📖 Read: Two Perspectives on the Same Event

The school board has announced that the old community garden next to Riverside School will be removed to build a new parking lot. Read what two different people think about this decision.

Perspective A: The School Board Chair

"We need the parking lot. Every morning, parents circle the block looking for a spot, creating traffic jams that make the intersection unsafe. The garden is lovely, but safety comes first. We'll plant a few trees in the new lot to keep some green space."

Perspective B: A Community Elder

"This garden has been here for thirty years. Kids learn to grow food here. Families gather here. My grandmother helped plant the first tomatoes. You can't replace that with a few trees in a parking lot. There must be another way to solve the traffic problem without destroying what connects us to this place."

Key Insight: Both people care about the community — but their experiences lead them to different conclusions. Understanding both perspectives doesn't mean agreeing with both. It means seeing why each person thinks the way they do.

⏸️ Pause and Check

What does each person value most? What evidence does each use?

Chair: Values safety — uses evidence about traffic jams and unsafe intersections. Elder: Values community connection and history — uses evidence about thirty years of learning, gathering, and cultural roots.

Activity 1

⚖️ Who Said It?

Sort each statement: does it belong to Perspective A (School Board Chair) or Perspective B (Community Elder)?
