Lesson 1

The Language of Wonder

Read a poem and an informational text on the same theme. Use comprehension strategies for both genres and discover how they create wonder differently.

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can use comprehension strategies to understand and respond to both poetry and informational text.
  • I can compare how a poem and an informational text explore the same theme.
  • I can identify how each text type creates a sense of wonder.
ComprehensionPoetryInformational Text
Figurative Language Words or phrases that mean something different from their literal meaning — used to create images or emphasis. Imagery Descriptive language that appeals to the senses — sight, sound, touch, taste, or smell. Tone The feeling or attitude conveyed by a text — such as curious, peaceful, urgent, or playful. Stanza A group of lines in a poem, similar to a paragraph in prose.

📖 Read: Two Ways of Seeing the Ocean

The same topic — the ocean — can be explored through poetry and through informational text. Each creates a different kind of wonder. Let's read both and compare.

Text A: Poem — "The Sea"

The sea does not care about your name. It pulls and pushes without permission, salting the air with ancient memory. Beneath, the jellyfish drift like lanterns in a city no one built, and the coral hums a song only the tide remembers. Come close. Listen. The ocean is telling you something you used to know.

— Original poem for classroom use

Text B: Informational — "Ocean Zones"

The ocean is divided into five zones based on depth. The sunlight zone (0–200m) receives the most light and supports the greatest diversity of life, including coral reefs, fish, and marine mammals. Below that, the twilight zone (200–1,000m) has dim light and is home to jellyfish and squid. The deepest zones are completely dark and feature extreme pressure and temperatures. Scientists estimate that more than 80% of the ocean remains unexplored.

Notice: Both texts are about the ocean — but the poem uses figurative language and imagery to create emotion, while the informational text uses facts and data to build understanding. Both create wonder, just in different ways.

⏸️ Pause and Check

How does the poem make you feel about the ocean? How does the informational text make you think about it? What's different about the kind of wonder each creates?

The poem creates emotional, mysterious wonder — it personifies the ocean and uses imagery like "lanterns" and "a song only the tide remembers." The informational text creates intellectual wonder — 80% unexplored! Both make us curious, but through different tools: feeling vs. fact.
