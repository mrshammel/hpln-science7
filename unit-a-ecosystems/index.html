Introduction to Ecosystems

Discover the living and non-living parts of ecosystems, how they interact, and how ecosystems meet the needs of all organisms.

STS-K.1STS-K.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can distinguish between biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) factors.
  • I can explain how biotic and abiotic factors interact and depend on each other.
  • I can describe how ecosystems meet the needs of living things.

✅ Success Criteria

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to check off all of these:

  • I can sort items into biotic and abiotic categories and explain why.
  • I can name at least 3 biotic and 3 abiotic factors and their roles.
  • I can explain what happens when one factor is removed from an ecosystem.
  • I can describe 5 things ecosystems provide to living organisms.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Hover over each term to see its definition.

🌎 EcosystemEcosystem: A community of living organisms interacting with each other and their non-living environment. Can be as small as a puddle or as large as a forest. 🌱 BioticBiotic: All living or once-living things in an ecosystem — plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, and even dead organisms. ☀️ AbioticAbiotic: All non-living physical and chemical factors — sunlight, water, temperature, soil, air, and minerals. 🏠 HabitatHabitat: The natural environment where an organism lives, providing food, water, shelter, and space. 🔄 InteractionInteraction: The way living and non-living things affect each other. Example: plants need sunlight (abiotic) to grow and provide food for animals (biotic). 🍽️ NutrientNutrient: Substances that organisms need for energy, growth, and repair. Plants get nutrients from soil; animals get them from food.

🌎 What Is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (plants, animals, fungi, bacteria) interacting with each other and their non-living environment (water, air, soil, sunlight). Ecosystems can be as small as a puddle or as large as the entire Earth!

Every ecosystem has two main types of components working together:

🌱

Biotic Factors

All living or once-living things: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, and their remains

☀️

Abiotic Factors

All non-living elements: sunlight, water, temperature, soil, air, minerals

💡 Key Idea: Every ecosystem depends on the interactions between biotic and abiotic factors. Remove one piece, and the entire system can be affected!

💡 Alberta Connection: Alberta has incredibly diverse ecosystems — from the boreal forests of the north to the grasslands of the south, the Rocky Mountain alpine regions, and wetlands like Elk Island National Park near Edmonton. Each has unique biotic and abiotic factors that create distinct communities of life.

📖 Video Summary — Ecosystem Ecology (Crash Course)

This video explains how ecosystems work as a whole — how energy flows and nutrients cycle through living and non-living parts of the environment.

🔑 Key Concepts

  • An ecosystem includes ALL the living organisms (biotic) AND non-living things (abiotic) in an area, working together as a system
  • Energy flows one way through an ecosystem: Sun → producers (plants) → primary consumers (herbivores) → secondary consumers (carnivores) → decomposers. Think of it like a one-way street — energy can never go backward!
  • Nutrients are recycled — unlike energy, nutrients like carbon and nitrogen go around in circles. When a deer eats grass, those nutrients move to the deer. When the deer dies, decomposers break it down, returning nutrients to the soil for new plants. It's like nature's recycling program!
  • Only about 10% of energy transfers from one level to the next — 90% is lost as heat. That's why there are always more plants than herbivores, and more herbivores than predators

💡 Examples & Analogies from the Video

  • The "factory" analogy: An ecosystem works like a factory — raw materials (nutrients) come in, energy powers the machines (organisms), and products and waste are created. The "waste" becomes raw materials again through decomposition
  • Trophic levels as a pyramid: Picture a pyramid — the wide base is plants (tons of energy), then herbivores (less energy), then carnivores (even less). A hawk at the top needs a LOT of mice below it, which need even MORE grain beneath them
  • Decomposers as nature's clean-up crew: Fungi and bacteria are like janitors — without them, dead things would pile up endlessly and nutrients would never return to the soil

📝 Important Vocabulary

  • Producers — organisms that make their own food (plants, algae) through photosynthesis
  • Consumers — organisms that eat other organisms for energy
  • Decomposers — organisms (fungi, bacteria) that break down dead matter and recycle nutrients
  • Trophic level — a feeding level in the ecosystem (producer → primary consumer → secondary consumer)

🤔 Think About It: If energy is lost at every level, why can't a food chain have 20 levels? There wouldn't be enough energy left to support animals at the top!

🔬 Biotic Factors: The Living World

Biotic factors include every living organism in an ecosystem — from the tallest tree to the tiniest bacterium.

🌳

Plants

Produce food through photosynthesis; provide oxygen and shelter

🦌

Animals

Consume plants or other animals; help with pollination and seed dispersal

🍄

Fungi

Decompose dead matter; recycle nutrients back into the soil

🦠

Bacteria

Break down organic materials; some fix nitrogen for plant use

Dead organisms are still classified as biotic because they were once living. Fallen leaves, dead animals, and decaying wood all still play crucial roles — decomposers break them down, returning nutrients to the soil. The distinction is about origin, not current state!

💧 Abiotic Factors: The Non-Living World

Abiotic factors are the non-living parts of an ecosystem. Even though they aren't alive, they are essential for the survival of every organism.

Abiotic FactorRole in the Ecosystem
☀️ SunlightEnergy source for photosynthesis; affects temperature and seasons
💧 WaterEssential for all life; habitat for aquatic organisms
🌡️ TemperatureDetermines which species can survive in an area
🪨 Soil & MineralsProvides nutrients for plants; habitat for organisms like worms
💨 Air (Atmosphere)Provides oxygen for animals and CO₂ for plants
🌧️ PrecipitationDelivers water to land ecosystems; shapes landscapes

📖 Video Summary — Ecological Relationships (Amoeba Sisters)

This video explores how biotic and abiotic factors interact within ecosystems, and how every part — living or non-living — plays a role in keeping the ecosystem balanced.

🔑 Key Concepts

  • Biotic factors are anything living OR once-living: plants, animals, bacteria, fungi, and even dead organisms like fallen leaves. The key test: "Was it ever alive?" If yes → biotic!
  • Abiotic factors are things that were NEVER alive: water, sunlight, temperature, soil minerals, wind, rocks. They set the "stage" for life
  • Biotic and abiotic factors depend on each other. Plants (biotic) need sunlight and water (abiotic) to grow. Animals (biotic) need shelter from weather (abiotic). Remove one, and the whole system is affected
  • Every ecosystem is unique because it has a different combination of biotic and abiotic factors — a desert has lots of sun but little water, while a rainforest has both

💡 Examples & Analogies from the Video

  • The "stage and actors" analogy: Abiotic factors are like the stage, lighting, and set design of a play — they create the environment. Biotic factors are the actors who perform on that stage. You need both for the show to work!
  • Dead leaf example: A fallen leaf on the ground is STILL biotic because it came from a living tree. It's not about whether something is alive right now, it's about whether it was ever alive
  • Temperature determines life: A polar bear thrives in the Arctic cold (abiotic), but would overheat in a desert. Abiotic factors act as "filters" deciding which species can live where
  • Removing one piece: If you remove all the water (abiotic) from a pond ecosystem, the fish and plants (biotic) can't survive — everything is connected like a web

📝 The 5 Needs All Ecosystems Provide

  • 🥗 Nutrients — food and minerals for growth
  • ⚡ Energy — sunlight that drives photosynthesis
  • 💦 Moisture — water for cellular processes
  • 🏠 Habitat — shelter and suitable living conditions
  • 🌬️ Gas Exchange — O₂ for animals, CO₂ for plants in a constant cycle

🤔 Think About It: Look outside your window — can you identify 3 biotic and 3 abiotic factors? How do they interact with each other?

Interactions & Interdependence

Explore how species depend on each other through symbiotic relationships, adaptations, and the connections between humans and their environments.

A.K.1

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can describe examples of interaction and interdependency within an ecosystem.
  • I can identify and explain the three types of symbiosis: mutualism, parasitism, and commensalism.
  • I can describe adaptations that help species survive in their relationships with other species.
  • I can identify changing relationships between humans and their environments over time.

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can classify real-world examples into mutualism, parasitism, or commensalism.
  • I can explain how removing one species affects others that depend on it.
  • I can give at least 2 examples of adaptations that support species interactions.
  • I can compare how Indigenous and modern communities relate to ecosystems differently.

📖 Key Vocabulary

🤝 SymbiosisSymbiosis: A close, long-term relationship between two different species living in the same area. 💚 MutualismMutualism: A symbiotic relationship where BOTH species benefit. Example: bees and flowers. 🦠 ParasitismParasitism: A relationship where one organism (the parasite) benefits while harming the other (the host). Example: ticks on a deer. 🪺 CommensalismCommensalism: A relationship where one species benefits and the other is neither helped nor harmed. Example: barnacles on a whale. 🔀 AdaptationAdaptation: A trait or behaviour that helps an organism survive in its environment. Example: camouflage, thick fur, sharp claws. 🔗 InterdependenceInterdependence: When two or more species rely on each other for survival — they are connected and affect each other.

🤝 What Is Symbiosis?

In every ecosystem, organisms don't live in isolation — they interact with each other constantly. When two different species live in a close, long-term relationship, scientists call it symbiosis. There are three main types:

💚

Mutualism

Both benefit. Bees get nectar from flowers; flowers get pollinated. Both win!

🦠

Parasitism

One benefits, one is harmed. A tick feeds on a deer's blood, weakening the deer.

🪺

Commensalism

One benefits, one is unaffected. Barnacles ride on whales — free travel, whale doesn't notice.

💡 Key Idea: Symbiosis shows that species don't just compete — many survive because of their relationships with other species.

⏸ Pause & Check

A clownfish lives among the stinging tentacles of a sea anemone. The anemone's stings protect the clownfish from predators. In return, the clownfish attracts prey for the anemone to eat. What type of symbiosis is this?

This is mutualism — both species benefit. The clownfish gets protection, and the anemone gets food.

🔀 Adaptations That Support Interactions

Many organisms have evolved adaptations — special traits — that help them interact with other species. These adaptations are key to survival.

🌸

Bright Flowers

Attract pollinators like bees, butterflies, and hummingbirds (mutualism)

🦔

Spines & Thorns

Protect plants from being eaten — thorns on roses, spines on cacti

🎭

Mimicry

Harmless species look like dangerous ones — hoverflies mimic wasps to avoid predators

🫎

Thick Fur

Alberta animals like moose grow thick winter coats to survive harsh winters

💡 Alberta Connection: The relationship between the Clark's nutcracker bird and whitebark pine trees in the Rocky Mountains is a perfect example of mutualism. The bird eats and caches the pine seeds, accidentally planting new trees across the mountainside.

Food Chains, Webs & Energy Flow

Trace how energy moves from the sun through producers, consumers, and decomposers in food chains and food webs.

A.K.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can identify producers, consumers, and decomposers in an ecosystem.
  • I can construct and interpret food chains and food webs.
  • I can explain how energy flows through trophic levels and why energy decreases at each level.
  • I can predict effects of changes to any part of a food web.

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can classify organisms as producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, or decomposers.
  • I can build a food chain with arrows showing the direction of energy flow.
  • I can explain why there are fewer top predators than producers in an ecosystem.
  • I can predict what happens when a species is removed from a food web.

📖 Key Vocabulary

🌱 ProducerProducer: An organism that makes its own food using sunlight (photosynthesis). Plants, algae, and some bacteria are producers. 🐰 Primary ConsumerPrimary Consumer: An herbivore that eats producers. Examples: rabbits, deer, grasshoppers. 🦊 Secondary ConsumerSecondary Consumer: A carnivore or omnivore that eats primary consumers. Examples: foxes, frogs, small birds. 🦅 Tertiary ConsumerTertiary Consumer: A top predator that eats secondary consumers. Examples: eagles, wolves, sharks. 🍄 DecomposerDecomposer: An organism that breaks down dead material and recycles nutrients. Examples: fungi, bacteria, earthworms. 🔗 Food ChainFood Chain: A linear sequence showing how energy passes from one organism to the next. Arrows point in the direction energy flows. 🕸️ Food WebFood Web: A network of interconnected food chains showing all the feeding relationships in an ecosystem. 📐 Trophic LevelTrophic Level: A step in a food chain. Level 1 = producers, Level 2 = primary consumers, etc.

🌱 Producers, Consumers & Decomposers

Every ecosystem has three key roles that organisms fill:

🌱

Producers

Make their own food via photosynthesis. They are the base of every food chain. Examples: grass, trees, algae.

🐰

Consumers

Get energy by eating other organisms. Herbivores eat plants; carnivores eat animals; omnivores eat both.

🍄

Decomposers

Break down dead organisms and waste, returning nutrients to the soil so producers can use them again.

💡 Key Idea: Without decomposers, dead matter would pile up and nutrients would never return to the soil — eventually, producers would run out of what they need to grow.

🔗 Food Chains & Trophic Levels

A food chain shows a single pathway of energy flow. The arrows always point in the direction energy moves — from the organism being eaten to the organism doing the eating.

☀️ Sun → 🌿 Grass → 🐰 Rabbit → 🦊 Fox → 🦅 Eagle
Trophic LevelRoleExampleEnergy Available
Level 1ProducerGrass100% (from sun)
Level 2Primary ConsumerRabbit~10%
Level 3Secondary ConsumerFox~1%
Level 4Tertiary ConsumerEagle~0.1%

💡 The 10% Rule: Only about 10% of energy is passed to the next trophic level. The rest is used for life processes (movement, growth, body heat) or lost as heat. This is why there are always more producers than top predators!

⏸ Pause & Check

Why are there usually more rabbits than eagles in an ecosystem?

Energy decreases at each trophic level (the 10% rule). There isn't enough energy at the top to support large populations of top predators. The base (producers) supports the most organisms.

🕸️ Food Webs: The Full Picture

Real ecosystems are much more complex than a single food chain. A food web shows all the interconnected feeding relationships. This is why removing one species can have ripple effects throughout the entire ecosystem.

💡 Alberta Example: In the boreal forest, wolves eat elk, which eat willows. When wolves were removed from some areas, elk overgrazed the willows, causing riverbank erosion and affecting fish habitat. One change cascaded through the whole web!

Lesson 4

Nutrient Cycles & Bioaccumulation

Explore how nutrients cycle through ecosystems, and what happens when pollutants build up in food chains.

A.K.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can describe how nutrients (carbon, water, nitrogen) cycle through ecosystems.
  • I can explain how pollutants accumulate and magnify through food chains (bioaccumulation and biomagnification).
  • I can explain why top predators are most affected by bioaccumulation.

📖 Key Vocabulary

🔄 Nutrient CycleNutrient Cycle: The continuous movement of nutrients (carbon, nitrogen, water) through living organisms, soil, water, and atmosphere. 💧 Water CycleWater Cycle: The continuous movement of water between Earth's surface and atmosphere through evaporation, condensation, and precipitation. 🏭 BioaccumulationBioaccumulation: The build-up of a substance (like a toxin) in an organism over time because it is absorbed faster than it is broken down. 📈 BiomagnificationBiomagnification: The increasing concentration of a toxin as it moves UP the food chain, with top predators having the highest levels.

🔄 How Nutrients Cycle

Unlike energy, which flows one way through an ecosystem (sun → producers → consumers), nutrients are recycled. The same carbon, nitrogen, and water atoms are used over and over.

🌫️

Carbon Cycle

CO₂ is absorbed by plants → eaten by animals → released through respiration → absorbed again. Decomposers also release carbon from dead matter.

💧

Water Cycle

Evaporation → condensation → precipitation → runoff/absorption → evaporation again. All living things need water to survive.

🧪

Nitrogen Cycle

Bacteria fix nitrogen from air → plants absorb it → animals eat plants → decomposers return nitrogen to soil. Essential for proteins and DNA.

💡 Key Idea: Nutrients cycle in loops; energy flows in one direction. This difference is fundamental to understanding ecosystem sustainability.

📺 Watch: Bioaccumulation

📺 Bioaccumulation & Biomagnification

🏭 Bioaccumulation & Biomagnification

When pollutants like pesticides or mercury enter an ecosystem, they don't just disappear. They accumulate in organisms and magnify up the food chain:

💧 Water (0.01 ppm) → 🌿 Algae (1 ppm) → 🐟 Small Fish (10 ppm) → 🐟 Large Fish (100 ppm) → 🦅 Eagle (1000 ppm)

💡 Alberta Connection: Mercury from industrial processes enters rivers and lakes. It bioaccumulates in Alberta fish, which is why there are fish consumption advisories for some northern lakes.

⏸ Pause & Check

Why are top predators like eagles and polar bears most affected by bioaccumulation?

Top predators eat many organisms that have already accumulated toxins. Each level magnifies the concentration, so by the time it reaches the top, the toxin levels are dangerously high.

Ecosystem Monitoring & Change

Learn how scientists and communities track ecosystem health, identify changes, and investigate causes of population change.

A.K.3

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can describe methods used to monitor ecosystem health (quadrats, transects, indicator species).
  • I can explain how and why populations change over time (succession, natural events, human activity).
  • I can interpret data about species populations and draw conclusions about ecosystem change.

📖 Key Vocabulary

📊 MonitoringMonitoring: Systematically observing and recording data about an ecosystem over time to track changes. 📐 QuadratQuadrat: A framed area (e.g., 1m × 1m) used to sample and count organisms in a specific location. 📏 TransectTransect: A line along which organisms are sampled at regular intervals to study changes across an area. 🐸 Indicator SpeciesIndicator Species: A species whose presence (or absence) tells us about ecosystem health. Example: frogs indicate clean water. 🌱 SuccessionSuccession: The gradual process by which ecosystems change and develop over time (e.g., bare rock → lichen → moss → grass → trees).

🔬 How Do Scientists Monitor Ecosystems?

📐

Quadrats

Place a frame on the ground and count every organism inside. Repeat in multiple locations for accuracy.

📏

Transects

Stretch a line across an area and sample at regular intervals. Great for studying gradual changes.

🐸

Indicator Species

Frogs, lichens, and certain invertebrates are sensitive to pollution — their presence means the ecosystem is healthy.

💡 Alberta Connection: Alberta Environment and Protected Areas uses monitoring programs like the Biodiversity Monitoring Institute (ABMI) to track species across the province's boreal forests, grasslands, and wetlands.

📺 Watch: Ecological Succession

📺 Ecological Succession

🌱 Ecological Succession

Succession is how ecosystems change over time. After a disturbance (fire, flood, or starting from bare rock), ecosystems slowly rebuild:

🪨 Bare Rock → 🟢 Lichens → 🌿 Mosses & Grasses → 🌲 Shrubs → 🌳 Mature Forest

Primary succession starts from scratch (bare rock). Secondary succession occurs after a disturbance (e.g., forest fire) where soil remains.

💡 Alberta Example: After the 2016 Fort McMurray wildfire, secondary succession began immediately — grasses and fireweed were the first plants to return, followed by shrubs and eventually trees.

Human Impact on Ecosystems

Investigate how human activities like pollution, deforestation, and resource extraction affect ecosystems — with a focus on Alberta.

A.K.3

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can identify specific ways human activities affect ecosystems (pollution, habitat loss, invasive species, resource extraction).
  • I can explain how these impacts affect food webs, nutrient cycles, and species populations.
  • I can analyze Alberta-specific examples of human environmental impact.

📖 Key Vocabulary

🏭 PollutionPollution: Introduction of harmful substances into the environment — chemical, noise, light, or thermal. 🪓 DeforestationDeforestation: Clearing large areas of forest for agriculture, mining, or development. 🐗 Invasive SpeciesInvasive Species: A non-native species introduced to an ecosystem that causes harm by outcompeting native species. ⛏️ Resource ExtractionResource Extraction: Removing natural resources (oil, minerals, timber) from ecosystems for human use.

🏭 Types of Human Impact

🏭

Pollution

Chemical runoff, air pollution, plastic waste — all disrupt organisms, food chains, and nutrient cycles.

🪓

Habitat Loss

Clearing forests, draining wetlands, and urban sprawl destroy the homes of countless species.

🐗

Invasive Species

Species like zebra mussels and purple loosestrife outcompete native species, disrupting food webs.

⛏️

Resource Extraction

Mining, oil sands, and logging alter landscapes and can contaminate water and soil.

💡 Alberta Focus: The oil sands region in northern Alberta is one of the largest industrial development areas in the world. While it provides economic benefits, it has significantly altered boreal forest ecosystems, affecting caribou habitat and water quality in the Athabasca River.

📖 Key Vocabulary

🌱 StewardshipStewardship: The responsible management and care of the environment for current and future generations. ♻️ SustainabilitySustainability: Meeting our current needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs. 🤔 Evidence-Based DecisionEvidence-Based Decision: Making choices based on scientific data and research rather than opinions or assumptions. 🤝 StakeholderStakeholder: A person or group with an interest in an environmental decision (e.g., farmers, scientists, First Nations, industry).

🌱 What Is Stewardship?

Stewardship means taking responsibility for the environment — not just using it, but actively caring for it so that it remains healthy for future generations.

🏡

Individual Actions

Reduce, reuse, recycle. Choose sustainable products. Reduce energy use. Plant native species.

🏫

Community Actions

Clean-up events, habitat restoration, citizen science programs, supporting local conservation.

🏛️

Government Actions

Protected areas, environmental regulations, pollution limits, species-at-risk legislation.

💡 Alberta Connection: Alberta has over 500 protected areas covering more than 12% of the province, including Banff National Park (Canada's first) and the Caribou Mountains Wildland Provincial Park.
