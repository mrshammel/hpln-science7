What is Heat? Thermal Energy & the Particle Model

Understand the difference between heat and temperature, how particles behave, and where heat comes from.

7-C.1 7-C.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can explain the difference between heat and temperature.
  • I can describe thermal energy using the particle model of matter.
  • I can identify different sources of heat energy.
  • I can explain how adding or removing heat affects particle movement and states of matter.

✅ Success Criteria

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to check off all of these:

  • I can explain that heat is a transfer of energy, not a thing.
  • I can describe how particles behave in solids, liquids, and gases.
  • I can give the bathtub vs. boiling water example to show heat ≠ temperature.
  • I can name at least 4 sources of heat energy.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Hover over each term to see its definition.

🔥 HeatHeat: The transfer of thermal energy from a warmer object to a cooler one. Measured in joules (J). 🌡️ TemperatureTemperature: A measure of the average kinetic energy (speed) of particles in a substance. Measured in °C or K. ⭐ Thermal EnergyThermal Energy: The total kinetic energy of all particles in a substance. Depends on temperature AND amount of matter. ⚡ Kinetic EnergyKinetic Energy: The energy of motion. Faster particles = more kinetic energy = higher temperature. ⚙️ Particle ModelParticle Model: A scientific model explaining that all matter is made of tiny particles that are always moving. 🔥 CombustionCombustion: A chemical reaction (burning) that releases heat and light energy. Example: burning wood or gas.

🤔 Heat vs. Temperature — What's the Difference?

Many people use "heat" and "temperature" interchangeably, but in science, they mean very different things!

ConceptHeatTemperature
What is it?The transfer of thermal energy from a warmer object to a cooler oneA measure of the average kinetic energy of particles
UnitsJoules (J) or caloriesDegrees Celsius (°C) or Kelvin (K)
AnalogyLike pouring water from one cup to anotherLike checking the water level in a cup
Key ideaHeat always flows from hot → coldTemperature tells you how hot or cold something is

💡 Key Distinction: A bathtub of warm water has MORE thermal energy (heat) than a cup of boiling water, even though the boiling water has a HIGHER temperature. Heat depends on both temperature AND the amount of matter.

⚛️ The Particle Model of Matter

The particle model of matter helps us understand heat and temperature at a microscopic level.

🧊

Solid

Particles vibrate in fixed positions. Closely packed. Low kinetic energy.

💧

Liquid

Particles slide past each other. More space. Moderate kinetic energy.

💨

Gas

Particles move freely and rapidly. Spread apart. High kinetic energy.

Adding heat increases the kinetic energy of particles — they move faster and spread further apart. This is why solids melt into liquids and liquids evaporate into gases. Removing heat does the opposite: particles slow down and move closer together.

🔋 Sources of Heat Energy

Heat energy comes from many sources. Humans have developed technologies to capture and control heat from these sources.

☀️

Solar (Sun)

The Sun is Earth's primary heat source. Solar panels convert it to electricity.

🔥

Chemical (Combustion)

Burning wood, gas, or coal releases stored chemical energy as heat.

⚡

Electrical

Electric heaters, stoves, and toasters convert electrical energy to heat.

🤲

Friction (Mechanical)

Rubbing hands together or braking a car converts motion to heat.

🌋

Geothermal

Heat from Earth's interior — used for heating homes in Iceland!

⚛️

Nuclear

Splitting atoms releases enormous amounts of thermal energy.

💡 Alberta Connection: Alberta uses multiple heat sources. Natural gas (chemical energy) heats most homes. Solar panels are growing in southern Alberta. Some communities near the Rocky Mountain foothills are exploring geothermal heating systems.

Answers: Solar, chemical, electrical, friction, geothermal, nuclear · They vibrate faster, gain energy, may break free (melt) · Gas particles lose energy and slow down, taking up less space

Heat Transfer: Conduction, Convection & Radiation

Learn the three ways heat moves and how we use insulation to control it.

7-C.37-C.4

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can describe conduction, convection, and radiation as three methods of heat transfer.
  • I can explain how conductors and insulators differ.
  • I can give real-world examples of each heat transfer method.
  • I can explain how insulation reduces heat loss in homes.

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can sort examples into conduction, convection, or radiation.
  • I can predict which materials make good conductors or insulators.
  • I can explain why a metal spoon heats up in soup (conduction).
  • I can describe how convection currents move heat in air and water.

📖 Key Vocabulary

🔧 ConductionConduction: Heat transfer through direct contact between particles. Particles vibrate and pass energy to neighbours. 🌀 ConvectionConvection: Heat transfer through the movement of fluids (liquids/gases). Hot fluid rises, cool fluid sinks, creating currents. ☀️ RadiationRadiation: Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves. Doesn't need particles — can travel through empty space. 🚧 ConductorConductor: A material that transfers heat easily (metals like copper, aluminum). Particles are tightly packed. 🧤 InsulatorInsulator: A material that slows heat transfer (wood, plastic, air, foam). Particles are far apart or loosely bonded. 🌀 Convection CurrentConvection Current: The circular flow pattern created when warm fluid rises and cool fluid sinks, transferring heat.

🔧 Conduction: Heat Through Touch

Conduction is heat transfer through direct contact. When fast-vibrating particles bump into slower neighbours, they pass energy along — like a chain of dominoes.

🍳

Example

A metal pan on a stove — heat travels from burner to pan to food

🦿

Conductors

Metals (copper, aluminum, steel) transfer heat quickly

🧤

Insulators

Wood, plastic, rubber, air — slow down heat transfer

💡 Key Idea: Conduction works best in solids because particles are packed closely together. That’s why a metal spoon in hot soup gets hot fast, but a wooden spoon stays cool.

⏸️ Pause and Check

  • Why does a metal spoon heat up faster than a wooden one in hot soup?
  • In conduction, do the particles themselves move to a new location?
  • Name two good conductors and two good insulators.

Answers: Metal has tightly packed particles that transfer vibrations quickly · No — particles vibrate in place and pass energy along · Conductors: copper, aluminum; Insulators: wood, plastic

🌀 Convection: Heat Through Movement

Convection occurs in fluids (liquids and gases). When a fluid is heated, particles move faster and spread apart — the warm fluid rises. Cooler, denser fluid sinks to replace it, creating circular convection currents.

🌬️

Air

Radiators heat air nearby → warm air rises → cool air sinks → room warms evenly

🌊

Water

Boiling water — hot water at the bottom rises, cool water sinks to be heated

💡 Alberta Connection: During Alberta’s cold winters (−40°C!), homes rely on forced-air furnaces and baseboard heaters that use convection. Hot air from vents rises and circulates around rooms, warming the entire house. Understanding convection helps Albertans design energy-efficient heating systems to reduce natural gas use.

☀️ Radiation: Heat Without Contact

Radiation is heat transfer through electromagnetic waves. Unlike conduction and convection, radiation does NOT need particles — it can travel through the vacuum of space. That’s how the Sun’s heat reaches Earth!

☀️

The Sun

Solar radiation travels 150 million km through space to warm Earth

🔥

Campfire

You feel warmth from a fire without touching it — infrared radiation

🖤

Dark Colours

Dark surfaces absorb more radiation; light surfaces reflect it

⏸️ Pause and Check

  • Which type of heat transfer does NOT need particles?
  • Why does warm air rise in a room?
  • Why would a black car feel hotter than a white car on a sunny day?

Answers: Radiation — it uses electromagnetic waves that travel through empty space · Warm air particles spread apart, making the air less dense — it floats up · Dark colours absorb more radiation than light colours, which reflect it

Thermal Expansion & Measurement

Discover how materials change size when heated or cooled, and how we use this knowledge in everyday technology.

C.K.2

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can explain thermal expansion and contraction using the particle model.
  • I can compare how solids, liquids, and gases respond to temperature changes.
  • I can describe real-world applications of thermal expansion.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Thermal Expansion: Materials increase in volume when heated — particles move faster and spread apart
Contraction: Materials decrease in volume when cooled — particles slow down and move closer
Expansion Joint: Gap built into bridges and sidewalks to allow for expansion without cracking
Bimetallic Strip: Two metals bonded together that bend when heated because they expand at different rates

🎬 Watch: Thermal Expansion

⏸️ Pause & Check: Why do gases expand more than solids when heated?

🔬 How Thermal Expansion Works

When you add thermal energy to a substance, its particles vibrate faster and push apart → the material expands.

StateExpansion AmountWhy?
SolidLeastParticles tightly packed, strong bonds
LiquidModerateParticles can slide apart more easily
GasMostParticles already far apart, free to spread

🌉 Real-World Applications

🌉 Expansion Joints: Bridges have gaps (expansion joints) so the metal can expand in summer heat without buckling. Alberta's Walterdale Bridge in Edmonton has these built in.
🌡️ Thermometers: Liquid in a thermometer expands as temperature rises, pushing up the tube — thermal expansion makes measurement possible.
🎈 Hot Air Balloons: Heating air makes it expand and become less dense → the balloon rises. Cooling air contracts → the balloon descends.
🔧 Bimetallic Strips: Two different metals bonded together bend when heated because they expand at different rates. Used in thermostats and fire alarms.

Heat Technologies Past & Present

Explore how humans have harnessed heat throughout history, from fire to solar panels.

C.K.1C.K.3

🎯 Learning Targets

  • I can describe historical and modern heat technologies and their purposes.
  • I can explain how devices generate, transfer, or control thermal energy.
  • I can compare passive and active solar heating systems.

📖 Key Vocabulary

Passive Solar: Capturing sun's heat without mechanical devices — south-facing windows, thermal mass
Active Solar: Using mechanical systems (pumps, fans) to collect and distribute solar heat
Thermostat: Device that regulates temperature by turning heating/cooling on and off
Combustion: Chemical reaction (burning) that releases thermal energy from fuel

🎬 Watch: History of Heat Technology

⏳ Heat Technology Through History

🔥 Ancient: Fire (100,000+ years ago) — cooking, warmth, protection. First controlled heat technology.
🏛️ Roman: Hypocaust — underfloor heating system using hot air. First central heating!
🚂 Industrial: Steam engine — converting heat to motion. Powered the Industrial Revolution.
🏠 Modern: Furnaces, heat pumps, thermostats, solar panels — precise heat control.
☀️ Future: Geothermal energy, hydrogen fuel cells, smart home heating with AI control.

☀️ Solar Heating: Passive vs Active

Feature☀️ Passive Solar⚙️ Active Solar
How it worksDesign captures heat naturallyMechanical devices collect & move heat
ExamplesSouth-facing windows, concrete floorsSolar water heaters, solar panels
CostLow (built into design)Higher (equipment needed)
MaintenanceAlmost noneRegular

📖 Key Vocabulary

Fossil Fuel: Coal, oil, natural gas — formed from ancient organisms, release CO₂ when burned
Renewable Energy: Solar, wind, geothermal, hydro — naturally replenished and sustainable
